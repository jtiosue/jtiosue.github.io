---
title: Lie exponential is matrix exponential for matrix Lie groups
comments: 30
---

{% include post-header.md %}


(22 July 2022) Consider $V$ is a tangent vector on $M$, and $M$ has coordinates $x^i$. Let $N$ be a manifold with coordinates $y^i$. Consider a diffeomorphism $f\colon M \to N$. Then the pushforward is $f_\ast\colon T_p M \to T_{f(p)}N$. Let $g\colon N \to \mathbb R$. Then

$$
(f_\ast V)(g) = V(g \circ f)
$$

We can get the components of $V$ by $V^i = V(x^i)$, where we view $x^i$ here as a function $M \to \mathbb R$. Indeed, if $V = V^j \partial_{x^j}$, then $V(x^i) = V^i$ as desired. Similarly, the components of $f_\ast V$ can be gotten by acting on $y^i$. Hence,

$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_\ast V)^i &= (f_\ast V)(y^i)\\
&= V(y^i \circ f)\\
&= V^j \partial_{x^j}(y^i \circ f)\\
&= V^j \partial_{x^j}(f^i(x))\\
&= V^j \frac{\partial f^i}{\partial x^j}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now consider a matrix Lie group, with left translation $L_g\colon G \to G :: h \mapsto gh$. Then the left invariant vector field defined by a $V \in T_e G$ is $X_V(g) = L_{g\ast}V$. From above, $(L_{g\ast}V)^i = V^j \partial_{x^j}(L_g^i)$. Here $L_g^i(x) = (gx)^i = g^i_j x^j$. Hence, $(L_{g\ast}V)^i = V^j g^{i}_j = (gV)^i$.

The one parameter subgroup generated by $V$, i.e. the homomorphism $\phi \colon \mathbb R \to G$, satisfies $\frac{d\phi(t)}{dt} = X_V(\phi(t))$ and $\phi(0) = e$. We know from above that $(X_V(g))^i = (gV)^i$. Hence, $\frac{d\phi^i(t)}{dt} = (\phi(t)V)^i$, or $\frac{d\phi}{dt} = \phi V$.

This differential equation with the initial condition is solved by $\phi(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(Vt)^n}{n!}$, which is precisely the matrix exponential $\phi(t) = \exp(Vt)$.

*Note that the indices, e.g. $\phi^i$, are referring to coordinate representations of $G$, hence matrix elements*.
